Sleep & Creativity

In a study published in the journal Sleep, researchers subjected a group of college students to 36 hours without sleep. The researchers then presented these students with a random image and asked them to respond with:

a) questions that struck them when they saw the image;
b) possible causes of the event occurring in the image;
c) possible consequences of the event.

The purpose of this exercise (as well as four others) was to test the students’ divergent thinking, or ability to produce unique ideas. What the researchers discovered, unsurprisingly, was that the test group subjected to sleeplessness performed significantly worse on the exercises compared to the control group which received an adequate amount of sleep. The test group students were unable to draw as many new connections, often repeating the same solutions and answers to new problems or situations which would otherwise require different approaches.

Using Processing, I was able to visually communicate what I learned into three iterations, which are explained in detail below.

Takeaway: Get a good night’s sleep, y'all.

Complementary Compliment Generator

As an early exercise in this larger user interaction project, I coded a compliment generator in Processing. The user begins by entering his or her name. Acquiring the user-input name, the sketch then generates random adjectives and nouns using arrayed strings to define the user.
