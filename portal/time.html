The Afternoon Slump

Documented on 04.22.2015

Time Location Activity
09:45 A   Getting ready for school

This is how you start your morning:

  1. Check your phone & see that you have thirty minutes before work starts.
  2. Open Spotify. Play that Matt & Kim song that's been stuck in your head.
  3. Crawl out of bed. This is the hardest part of your day.
  4. Pick a shirt from your drawer and pair of jeans from the top of your laundry basket – both of these will be black or dark, dark grey.
  5. Brush your hair because it's the least you can do.
  6. Choose between strappy sandals and constricting lace ups.
  7. Choose sandals 6 out of 7 days a week.
  8. Pack your backpack.
  9. Get on your bike.
  10. Go, go, go.

Wake-up music: "Get It" by Matt & Kim

10:00 A   Biking to campus

This is your commute to campus:

  1. Wave to your housemate who's walking to the bus stop.
  2. Almost get clotheslined by someone opening their car door.
  3. Lift yourself off your bike as you go over the train tracks. Your tires have already popped three times this year because of those tracks.
  4. Hit every red light on your way through downtown.
  5. Make it to campus.
  6. Be hyperaware of every biker beside you who refuses to use turn signals.
  7. Find an empty spot across from the MU.
  8. Lock your bike.

Biking-fast music: "I Always Knew" by The Vaccines

10:15 A   Settling into work

This is your first fifteen minutes at work:

  1. Check your email. You will never not have email.
  2. Checkmark the promotional emails, the listserv emails. Mark them as read.
  3. Star the emails from addresses you should get back to.
  4. Visit medium.com.
  5. Find an interesting article. Or two. Or three.
  6. Bookmark all the articles!
  7. Decide it's time to get to work.

Today-may-just-be-okay music: "When They Fight, They Fight" by The Generationals

10:30 A   Getting down to work

This is your actual first fifteen minutes at work:

  1. Check your email again. You have two new ones.
  2. Go back to the starred emails. Consider the graphics your clients are requesting.
  3. Consult your very-meticulously designed and updated spreadsheet.
  4. Decide who's the best designer on your team for each project.

Ease-into-it music: "Floating Vibes" by Surfer Blood

10:45 A   Sending emails (ad infinitum)

This is a summary of your job:

  1. Send emails detailing new projects to the assigned designer.
  2. Send more emails detailing more projects to more designers.
  3. Send emails to follow up on on-going projects.
  4. Send emails to arrange meetings with clients to get more projects.
  5. Keep your email tab open because your job is 80% emails.

Stay-dreamy music: "After the Moment" by Craft Spells

11:00 A   Designing a thing

This is one of those rare projects where you are actually designing:

  1. As we've established, your job mainly consists of emails / spreadsheets / meetings.
  2. That is why this project – this opportunity to redesign a website from back-end to front, from the user's experience to what they see on the screen – is so special!
  3. You, fingers swiping across the trackpad, open up Photoshop.
  4. You set the image size to 1280 x 720, your resolution to 72dpi.
  5. You make a twelve-column grid.
  6. You're ready.

Get-elevated music: "Lifted Up (1985)" by Passion Pit

11:15 A   Remembering what you forgot

This is how you go about designing:

  1. You make a checklist of style guide components.
  2. You place checkboxes next to fonts – their sizes and leading and web-compatibility; to button styles in every state imaginable; to forms active, inactive, completed.
  3. You make an extensive list, and check it twice.
  4. You feel accomplished.
  5. You then remember you promised your dad you would call him today.

Staying-in-the-zone music: "Breathing Underwater" by Metric

11:30 A   Finally calling your Dad

This is how your phone call with your Dad goes:

  1. He is so happy you could fit them into your schedule. He says this sarcastically, referring to your Mom and himself.
  2. You make an irritated face he cannot see.
  3. He asks whether you know if you will be able to graduate this Spring.
  4. You tell him you'll find out this Wednesday, after your meeting with your advisor.
  5. He says he'll call again on Wednesday.
  6. Okay, you say, then ask him about his leg. How is it treating him? Is he able to drive?
  7. He tells you it still gives him pain, but he's seeing a physical therapist twice a month.
  8. That's good, you affirm.
  9. He's able to drive but not far distances. He probably won't be able to visit you anytime soon.
  10. You tell him not to worry about it.
  11. He asks if you've talked to your sister.
  12. You have. The two of you talked on the phone a few days ago. She's developing a baby bump, she says. She takes pictures of herself, her baby bump and baby books, and posts them on Instagram.
  13. He does not understand Instagram.
  14. It's pretty easy, you tell him.
  15. He assures you it is not.
  16. Okay, you say. Maybe you can show him next time.
  17. He says that would be nice. Your sister doesn't tell him much. It's very hard to get in contact with her, he says. She doesn't return his calls or texts.
  18. You reassure him that she's probably really busy.
  19. He knows.
  20. You let him know you have to go.
  21. Call home more often, he asks.
  22. You'll try.
  23. You always try.

Your listen to the tiredness in your father's voice and short sighs.

11:45 A   Catching up with the world

This is your social media cycle:

  1. You sign into Twitter.
  2. You learn that the police have murdered another Black man in Baltimore.
  3. You learn that people are in the streets, protesting.
  4. You read the news outlets labeling protests riots, protestors thugs.
  5. You understand that, to a vast majority of white America, property is more important than Black lives.
  6. You think about that. You think about its implications.

The-world-is-burning music: "Holland, 1945" by Neutral Milk Hotel

12:00 P   Returning to work

This is your downward spiral:

  1. You are significantly less motivated to continue designing this style guide.
  2. In the greater scheme of things, you think. How is this helping anyone?
  3. Design is petty, you think. It's a selfish, self-absorbed career choice.
  4. You make a few more buttons to pixel perfection.
  5. You feel the exhaustion kicking it.

Getting-by music: "I'll Believe In Anything" by Wolf Parade

12:15 P   Feeling the weight of everything

This is you:

  1. Responsiblities.
  2. Ugh.

Pulling-you-through music: "The Beast" by Cataldo

12:30 P   Trying to stay awake

This is the only thing your brain can process:

  1. Maybe you should've slept more than an hour and a half last night.
  2. You really don't appreciate your bed enough.

You-should-turn-up-the-volume music: "Harlem" by New Politics

12:45 P   Thinking about thinking

This is your next big decision:

  1. You feel like caffeine would give you a much needed productivity boost.
  2. But you don't want to spend money on something you could make at home for free.
  3. But home is so far.
  4. But coffee here is not very good.
  5. But it is close.
  6. And you are close to KO'ing.
  7. And you have a meeting in 20 minutes.
  8. Coffee it is.

Play-the-most-upbeat-music-possible music: "Little Games" by The Colourist

01:00 P   Biking to the Student Community Center

This is an uneventful bike ride.

You do see a smushed squirrel, recognizable only by the fluff of its tail.
01:15 P   Meeting

This is confidential.

01:30 P   Meeting

This is confidential.

01:45 P   Being free

These are your plans for the rest of the day:

  1. Ride your bike home.
  2. Let that internship in Virginia know you're taking their offer!
  3. Let the other internship in San Diego know you're not.
  4. Sleep.
  5. Sleep.
  6. Sleep.

Glorious-biking music: "Everything's A Ceiling" by Death Cab For A Cutie
